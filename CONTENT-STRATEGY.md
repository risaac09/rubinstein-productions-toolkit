# Rubinstein Productions Toolkit — Content Strategy
### The Liver Processes and Expresses
*April 2026*

---

## The Organizing Metaphor

The Digital Liver has two functions: it **processes** (filters, metabolizes, decides what to keep) and it **expresses** (returns the metabolized result to the body, to the world).

The toolkit IS this metaphor in action:
- **Processing:** Methodology, evaluation frameworks, Digital Liver theory, the Fractal Enclosure — these are the filtering organs. They take raw experience and institutional noise and metabolize them into something honest.
- **Expressing:** CLI tools, prompt stack, open source publishing, the camera-shipping model — these are the output. Metabolized experience made public, shareable, replicable.

Every piece of content should embody this dual function. Show the processing AND the expressing. The "how I built this" and the "here, use it."

## Two Audiences, One Hook

### Audience 1: Practitioners (the GitHub crowd)
Solo consultants, nonprofit evaluators, documentary filmmakers, AI-assisted practitioners, indie devs.
**What they want:** Tools that work. A stack they can steal from. Proof that a solo practitioner can build real infrastructure without a team.
**Where they live:** GitHub, Hacker News, Indie Hackers, Dev Twitter/X, niche Substacks.
### Audience 2: Funders + Partners (the Say Why crowd)
Foundation program officers, social impact leaders, community health organizations, potential fiscal sponsors.
**What they want:** Evidence that this methodology is real, rigorous, and replicable. Proof that it's operationalized, not just theorized.
**Where they live:** LinkedIn, SSIR, Chronicle of Philanthropy, grant portals, conference hallways.

**The bridge:** Both audiences are drawn to the same thing — someone who built the infrastructure to do honest, relational work at a professional level, and then shared it because they wanted to. The craft IS the hook. The generosity is implicit.

## Content Pillars

### Pillar 1: "Building in Public" (Process)
Show the work of building the toolkit — the decisions, the tradeoffs, the philosophy encoded in the code.

**Formats:**
- GitHub commit messages that tell a story
- LinkedIn posts: "Here's what I learned building a grant pipeline in bash scripts instead of Airtable"
- Thread-style posts: "I open-sourced my entire consultancy stack. Here's what's in it and why."
- Short essays: "Why I put a nervous-system check in my CRM"

**Cadence:** 1-2 posts/week

### Pillar 2: "Here, Use It" (Express)
Show what the tools do and how anyone can use them. Practical, generous, zero gatekeeping.
**Formats:**
- GitHub README (already done — this IS content)
- Tutorial posts: "How to build an Obsidian-native outreach pipeline in 30 minutes"
- Tool spotlights: "The Nomadic Indicators Codebook — how to measure what evaluation frameworks flatten"
- Video walkthroughs: Screen recording of the CLI tools in action (iPhone or screen capture, raw, no polish)

**Cadence:** 1-2 posts/week

### Pillar 3: "The Philosophical Spine" (Deep Process)
Connect the tools back to the theory — Digital Liver, Fractal Enclosure, relational ontology, information metabolism. This is where the deeper thinking lives, but it earns its place by being grounded in the practice.

**Formats:**
- Long-form essays (Substack or LinkedIn articles): "Information Metabolism: Why Your Productivity System is Making You Sick"
- Position papers: The Extraction Problem (already written — publish it)
- Academic-accessible pieces for SSIR, Health Affairs Blog
- Podcast appearances / guest posts (leverage the toolkit as a hook)

**Cadence:** 1-2 per month

### Pillar 4: "Say Why in Practice" (Express into the world)
Document the Say Why initiative as it launches — the grant applications, the fiscal sponsorship journey, the first participants, the first videos.
**Formats:**
- LinkedIn updates: "We just applied for fiscal sponsorship. Here's what that process looks like for a solo practitioner."
- Behind-the-scenes: "What it actually takes to ship a camera to a community health worker"
- Participant stories (with consent): short clips, written reflections
- Grant journey transparency: "We applied for RWJF. Here's the brief proposal. Here's what we learned."

**Cadence:** As it happens — 2-4 posts/month once Say Why launches

## Launch Sequence (First 30 Days)

### Week 1: The Drop
- **Day 1:** LinkedIn post — "I just open-sourced my entire consultancy stack." Link to GitHub. Keep it short. Let the README do the work. Confession hook format.
- **Day 2:** Twitter/X thread — Walk through the repo structure. Tag it #buildinpublic.
- **Day 3:** Second LinkedIn post — Spotlight one tool. The outreach CLI. "I built a CRM in bash scripts because I didn't want to pay for one and I wanted it to live in my notes app."
- **Day 4-5:** Engage. Reply to comments. Don't promote. Respond to what's alive.

### Week 2: The Practice
- **Day 8:** LinkedIn post — the Evaluation Framework. "Here's how I measure relational quality alongside deliverables." The Bilingual Dashboard concept.
- **Day 10:** Publish "The Extraction Problem" as a LinkedIn article or Substack. Let it live as a philosophical piece. Don't position it as a manifesto — present it as thinking.
- **Day 11:** Tool spotlight — the prompt stack. "I have 15 AI skill files that encode my methodology. Here's how they work together."
- **Day 12:** Cross-post to Hacker News or Indie Hackers: "I'm a solo facilitator who encoded my entire practice into an AI-assisted stack. Here's the repo."

### Week 3: The Depth
- **Day 15:** LinkedIn post — "I applied for fiscal sponsorship yesterday. Here's what that process looks like for a solo practitioner." Transparency content.
- **Day 17:** Long-form piece — the Digital Liver metaphor. Information metabolism. The craft of building a second brain that actually produces output.
- **Day 19:** Short video (iPhone, 60 seconds) — "Why I put the theory of change in the GitHub repo."
### Week 4: The Invitation
- **Day 22:** LinkedIn post — "If you're a solo consultant building your own stack, here's what I wish I'd known." Lessons learned. Generous. No pitch.
- **Day 24:** Announce Say Why — "I'm building a nonprofit initiative that uses facilitation and film to help communities tell their own stories. Here's the theory of change."
- **Day 26:** Toolkit update — push new content to GitHub. Show the living nature of the repo.
- **Day 28:** End-of-month reflection. What landed. What didn't. Was the expression honest? Did the cadence feel nourishing or depleting?

## Naming + Positioning

**Toolkit name:** Rubinstein Productions Toolkit
**Tagline for the repo:** "The complete operational stack behind a solo facilitation and film practice"
**Tagline for content:** "The liver processes and expresses"

**The tone:** I built this for myself. It works. Here's how. Take what's useful.

**The narrative arc across all content:**
1. Here's a practice I built because I love building it (the toolkit, build-in-public)
2. Here's the thinking underneath it (Digital Liver, Fractal Enclosure, evaluation philosophy)
3. Here's how you can use it (open source, templates, guides)
4. Here's how communities can use it (Say Why, grant-funded work)

That arc moves from personal craft → shared methodology → public infrastructure → collective practice. Each piece of content lives somewhere on that arc.

---

## What NOT to Publish

- Quantum Mirror daily entries (private practice)
- Prospect/funder data (competitive + relational privacy)
- Pricing worksheets (business-sensitive)
- Client communications (confidential)
- Personal financial data
- Gene Keys / Human Design specifics (private)

**The rule: publish the methodology, not the diary. Publish the tools, not the data they hold.**
