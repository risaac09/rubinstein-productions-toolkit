# Rubinstein Productions Toolkit — Content Strategy
### The Liver Processes and Expresses
*April 2026*

---

## The Organizing Metaphor

The Digital Liver has two functions: it **processes** (filters, metabolizes, decides what to keep) and it **expresses** (returns the metabolized result to the body, to the world).

The toolkit IS this metaphor in action:
- **Processing:** Methodology, evaluation frameworks, Digital Liver theory, the Fractal Enclosure — these are the filtering organs. They take raw experience and institutional noise and metabolize them into something honest.
- **Expressing:** CLI tools, prompt stack, open source publishing, the camera-shipping model — these are the output. Metabolized experience made public, shareable, replicable.

Every piece of content we publish from the toolkit should embody this dual function. It should show the processing AND the expressing. The "how I built this" and the "here, use it."

## Two Audiences, One Philosophy

### Audience 1: Practitioners (the GitHub crowd)
Solo consultants, nonprofit evaluators, documentary filmmakers, AI-assisted practitioners, indie devs.
**What they want:** Tools that work. A stack they can steal from. Proof that a solo practitioner can build real infrastructure without a team.
**Where they live:** GitHub, Hacker News, Indie Hackers, Dev Twitter/X, niche Substacks.

### Audience 2: Funders + Partners (the Say Why crowd)
Foundation program officers, social impact leaders, community health organizations, potential fiscal sponsors.
**What they want:** Evidence that this methodology is real, rigorous, and replicable. Proof that anti-extractive isn't just a philosophy — it's operationalized.
**Where they live:** LinkedIn, SSIR, Chronicle of Philanthropy, grant portals, conference hallways.
**The bridge:** Both audiences are drawn to the same thing — someone who built the infrastructure to do honest, relational work at a professional level, and then gave it away. The philosophy IS the hook for both. The format differs.

## Content Pillars

### Pillar 1: "Building in Public" (Process)
The liver processing. Show the work of building the toolkit — the decisions, the tradeoffs, the philosophy encoded in the code.

**Formats:**
- GitHub commit messages that tell a story (not "updated README" but "added sacral check to outreach CLI because outreach without resonance is spam")
- LinkedIn posts: "Here's what I learned building a grant pipeline in bash scripts instead of Airtable"
- Thread-style posts: "I open-sourced my entire consultancy stack. Here's what's in it and why."
- Short essays: "Why I put a nervous-system check in my CRM"

**Cadence:** 1-2 posts/week from this pillar

### Pillar 2: "The Anti-Extractive Stack" (Express)
The liver expressing. Show what the tools do and how anyone can use them. Practical, generous, zero gatekeeping.

**Formats:**
- GitHub README (already done — this IS content)
- Tutorial posts: "How to build an Obsidian-native outreach pipeline in 30 minutes"
- Tool spotlights: "The Nomadic Indicators Codebook — how to measure what evaluation frameworks flatten"
- Video walkthroughs: Screen recording of the CLI tools in action (iPhone or screen capture, raw, no polish)

**Cadence:** 1-2 posts/week from this pillar

### Pillar 3: "The Philosophical Spine" (Deep Process)
The liver's relationship to the body it serves. Connect the tools back to the theory — Digital Liver, Fractal Enclosure, relational ontology, information metabolism.

**Formats:**
- Long-form essays (Substack or LinkedIn articles): "Information Metabolism: Why Your Productivity System is Making You Sick"
- Position papers: The Extraction Problem (already written — publish it)
- Academic-accessible pieces for SSIR, Stanford Social Innovation Review, Health Affairs Blog
- Podcast appearances / guest posts (leverage the toolkit as a hook)

**Cadence:** 1-2 per month from this pillar
### Pillar 4: "Say Why in Practice" (Express into the world)
The liver returning metabolized experience to the communities that need it. Document the Say Why initiative as it launches — the grant applications, the fiscal sponsorship journey, the first participants, the first videos.

**Formats:**
- LinkedIn updates: "We just applied for fiscal sponsorship. Here's what that process looks like for a solo practitioner."
- Behind-the-scenes: "What it actually takes to ship a camera to a community health worker in Detroit"
- Participant stories (with consent): short clips, written reflections, the "before institutional script / after honest expression" contrast
- Grant journey transparency: "We applied for RWJF. Here's the brief proposal. Here's what we learned."

**Cadence:** As it happens — 2-4 posts/month once Say Why launches

## The Content Flywheel

```
Toolkit (GitHub)
    ↓ generates
Build-in-Public Content (LinkedIn, Substack)
    ↓ attracts
Practitioners who use + fork the tools
    ↓ creates
Social proof + community
    ↓ strengthens
Grant applications (Say Why)
    ↓ funds
Community engagements
    ↓ produces
Participant stories + evaluation data
    ↓ feeds back into
Toolkit updates + new content
```

The flywheel works because every node feeds every other node. The GitHub repo isn't separate from the content strategy — it IS the content strategy. Publishing the toolkit generates the content. The content attracts the community. The community validates the grants. The grants fund the work. The work produces the stories. The stories update the toolkit.

## Launch Sequence (First 30 Days)

### Week 1: The Drop
- **Day 1:** LinkedIn post — "I just open-sourced my entire consultancy stack." Link to GitHub. Keep it short. Let the README do the work. Use the Confession hook format from the creative strategy.
- **Day 2:** Twitter/X thread — Walk through the repo structure. Tag it #buildinpublic. Name the anti-extractive principle.
- **Day 3:** Second LinkedIn post — Spotlight one tool. The outreach CLI with the sacral check. "I built a CRM that asks you if reaching out feels like a yes or a should."
- **Day 4-5:** Engage. Reply to comments. Don't promote. Respond to what's alive.
### Week 2: The Philosophy
- **Day 8:** Publish "The Extraction Problem" as a LinkedIn article or Substack. This is your position paper. It grounds everything.
- **Day 9:** LinkedIn post pulling one paragraph from the paper — the "digital liver" metaphor. Link to the full piece.
- **Day 11:** Tool spotlight — the Evaluation Framework. "Here's how I measure relational quality alongside deliverables. The Bilingual Dashboard."
- **Day 12:** Cross-post the Substack piece to Hacker News or Indie Hackers with the frame: "I built an anti-extractive consultancy and open-sourced the whole stack."

### Week 3: The Practice
- **Day 15:** LinkedIn post — "I applied for fiscal sponsorship yesterday. Here's what that looks like for a solo practitioner with no board and no history." Transparency content.
- **Day 17:** Tool spotlight — the prompt stack. "I have 15 AI skill files that encode my methodology, brand voice, and evaluation logic. Here's how they work together."
- **Day 19:** Short video (Yapper format, iPhone, 60 seconds) — "Why I put the theory of change in the GitHub repo." The intellectual honesty of publishing your grant infrastructure publicly.

### Week 4: The Invitation
- **Day 22:** LinkedIn post — "If you're a solo consultant building your own stack, here's what I wish I'd known." Lessons learned. Generous. No pitch.
- **Day 24:** Announce Say Why — "I'm building a nonprofit initiative that uses facilitation and film to help communities say why. Here's the theory of change."
- **Day 26:** Toolkit update — push new content to GitHub. Reference it in a "what's new" post. Show the living nature of the repo.
- **Day 28:** End-of-month reflection. What landed. What didn't. The Nomadic indicators of the content itself — did it feel nourishing or depleting? Was the expression honest? Bilingual check-in, published.

## Naming + Positioning

**Toolkit name:** Rubinstein Productions Toolkit
**Tagline for the repo:** "An open-source stack for anti-extractive consultancy"
**Tagline for content:** "The liver processes and expresses"

**The narrative arc across all content:**
1. The system is extractive (the Fractal Enclosure, the Extraction Problem)
2. You can metabolize what it gives you (the Digital Liver, information metabolism)
3. Here's how I did it (the toolkit, the build-in-public)
4. Here's how you can do it (the open source, the templates, the guides)
5. Here's how communities can do it (Say Why, the grant-funded work)

That arc moves from diagnosis → personal practice → public infrastructure → collective healing. Each piece of content lives somewhere on that arc.

## What NOT to Publish

- Quantum Mirror daily entries (private practice)
- Prospect/funder data (competitive + relational privacy)
- Pricing worksheets (business-sensitive)
- Client communications (confidential)
- Personal financial data
- Gene Keys / Human Design specifics (private)

The rule: **publish the methodology, not the diary. Publish the tools, not the data they hold.**
