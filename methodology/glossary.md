# Glossary of Key Terms — Ethical, Nervous-System Aware Videography

---

## **A–C**

### **Agency Retention**

The principle that participants maintain control over their narrative, image, and story use. Derived from participatory research ethics and Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985).

### **Authenticity**

A state of congruence between internal experience and external expression; the alignment of nervous-system safety, psychological coherence, and communicative integrity. Anchored in Edmondson’s work on psychological safety and Porges’ ventral vagal regulation model.

### **Behavioral Extraction**

A process within surveillance capitalism where human behavior is captured, analyzed, and monetized as data (Zuboff, 2019). In traditional videography, extraction occurs when footage is taken without reciprocal benefit to subjects.

### **Calm Companion Mode**

A facilitation stance where the filmmaker or facilitator regulates their own physiology to offer nonverbal safety cues—tone, pacing, and stillness—that calm the interviewee’s nervous system (Porges, 2011; Siegel, 2012).

### **Co-Regulation**

The reciprocal process where two or more nervous systems synchronize through nonverbal cues such as gaze, tone, and breath. Essential to maintaining safety during interviews and collaborative filming.

### **Consent Loop**

A dynamic process of continuous consent rather than one-time approval, revisited at natural moments throughout filming. Draws on participatory research models and trauma-informed practice.

### **Cognitive Load**

The mental effort required to manage self-monitoring and performance under observation. Elevated cognitive load during filming increases stress hormones and reduces authenticity (Sweller, 1988).

---

## **D–H**

### **Deepfakes / Epistemological Collapse**

Synthetic video content that undermines trust in visual evidence, leading to erosion of photographic truth (Barthes, 1980; Zuboff, 2019). Raises need for embodied, relational authenticity as new trust anchor.

### **Dorsal Vagal State**

A polyvagal response characterized by immobilization, shutdown, or dissociation in response to perceived threat. Common in subjects who feel overexposed or objectified by the camera (Porges, 2011).

### **Facilitation Before Technology**

A production principle asserting that relational and psychological preparation precedes technical setup. Grounded in Layer 2 “Permission-Based Framing.”

### **Freeze Response**

A stress-induced immobility state where the body halts expressive movement to minimize threat exposure. Common under evaluative observation or intense lighting conditions.

### **Grounding**

Any sensory or breath-based activity that restores awareness to the present moment and stabilizes physiological arousal. Examples include slow breathing or tactile anchoring.

---

## **I–N**

### **Intent Alignment**

The process of establishing shared purpose before filming to synchronize expectations, ethical stance, and emotional tone. Derived from facilitation and systems design practice.

### **Mirror Neurons**

Neural systems responsible for empathy and imitation; activated during both observation and action (Gallese, 2001). Their activation without relational reciprocity can cause cognitive overload during recording.

### **Mutual Witnessing**

The act of shared observation where both filmmaker and subject remain present to one another’s humanity. Contrasts with extractive observation; central to Haraway’s ethics of relational seeing (1991).

### **Neuroception**

An unconscious evaluation mechanism by which the nervous system detects cues of safety or danger (Porges, 2011). Camera presence often triggers threat neuroception until trust is established.

### **Nervous-System Awareness**

The conscious tracking of physiological states—heart rate, breath, muscle tension—to regulate presence and authenticity during production.

---

## **O–R**

### **Oxytocin Pathway**

A neurochemical system associated with bonding and trust. Activated through eye contact, gratitude, and empathetic resonance; key to post-filming integration (Lieberman, 2013).

### **Participatory Filming**

A collaborative approach where subjects contribute to story design, consent framing, and editing decisions. Derived from participatory action research and community-based documentation models.

### **Performance Anxiety Cascade**

A feedback loop of nervous-system activation caused by perceived judgment on camera. Manifests as elevated cortisol, self-monitoring, and speech disruption.

### **Polyvagal Theory**

A framework by Stephen Porges explaining how three branches of the vagus nerve—ventral, sympathetic, dorsal—govern social connection and defense responses.

### **Psychological Safety**

A shared belief that one can express oneself without risk of punishment or humiliation (Edmondson, 1999). In filming, it enables subjects to speak truthfully rather than performatively.

---

## **S–Z**

### **Self-Authorship**

A developmental concept where individuals define their own identity and values, essential for authentic storytelling (Kegan, 1982). Applied here to narrative sovereignty.

### **Surveillance Capitalism**

An economic logic that commodifies human experience as behavioral data for prediction and control (Zuboff, 2019). Filmmaking can resist this logic by emphasizing reciprocity and participant agency.

### **Sympathetic Activation**

A physiological state of mobilization (“fight or flight”) triggered by perceived threat or evaluation. Reduces spontaneity and expressive range on camera.

### **Ventral Vagal State**

The polyvagal system’s social engagement mode—characterized by calm attention, prosody, and emotional expressiveness. Optimal state for authentic communication.

### **Witnessing vs. Capturing**

The distinction between documenting as presence (witnessing) and recording as possession (capturing). Central ethical divide between extractive and facilitative filming practices.
