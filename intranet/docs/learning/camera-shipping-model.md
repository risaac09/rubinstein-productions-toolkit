# The Camera-Shipping Model

## What It Is

Instead of sending a production crew to a participant, RP ships a pre-configured camera kit to the participant. They film themselves, in their own space, on their own schedule, without crew presence. The footage is returned along with the equipment. The facilitator's role shifts from director to editor.

## Why It Matters

The camera-shipping model is not a budget optimization. It is a methodological choice rooted in the same principles that govern the facilitation:

**Participant authorship.** When a crew shows up, the participant becomes a subject — someone who is filmed. When a camera arrives in a box, the participant becomes an author — someone who films. This shift in power dynamics produces fundamentally different footage.

**Environmental authenticity.** A crew changes the room. The camera-shipping model lets participants film in their actual environment, with their actual lighting, wearing their actual clothes. The result is rawer and truer.

**Temporal freedom.** Participants can film when the moment is right — not when the crew is scheduled. Some of the best footage comes at 11pm when someone can't sleep and finally says what they've been holding.

**Reduced surveillance anxiety.** Even the most sensitive crew creates a surveillance dynamic. A camera on a tripod, alone in someone's living room, does not.

---

## When to Use It

The camera-shipping model works best for:

- Individual participant stories (one person filming themselves)
- Environments where a crew would disrupt the natural dynamic
- Geographically distributed participants (multiple cities, rural areas)
- Situations requiring high intimacy and low performance pressure
- Budget-constrained projects that still need production quality

It works less well for:

- Multi-person scenes requiring coordination
- Events or gatherings (conferences, meetings, ceremonies)
- Situations requiring real-time directorial decisions
- Participants who are uncomfortable with technology (offer on-site as alternative)

## The Kit

See [Equipment & Logistics](../operations/equipment-logistics.md) for full kit contents and shipping protocol.

Key design principles for the kit:

- **Zero friction.** Everything should work out of the box. Pre-configure the camera, pre-install apps, pre-charge batteries. The participant should not need to troubleshoot anything.
- **Clear instructions.** Include printed Camera Kit Guide (`06-camera-kit-guide.docx`). Written instructions survive where verbal ones don't.
- **Return path.** Pre-paid return label included. Make returning the kit as easy as receiving it.
- **Support availability.** Participant should have a direct text/phone line to Isaac for technical questions during the filming window.

---

## Orientation Call

Before filming begins, offer a 15-minute orientation call (phone or video). This is optional but recommended, especially for first-time participants.

Cover:

1. How to use the tripod and microphone
2. Lighting basics (face a window, avoid backlighting)
3. Framing (head and shoulders, eyes in upper third)
4. Sound check (test the lav mic, film a 10-second clip and play it back)
5. What to film: "Talk like you're talking to a friend. The messy version is what we want."
6. Permission to delete: "If you film something and don't like it, delete it. You're the author."

## Quality Considerations

**Audio is king.** The lavalier microphone is the most critical piece of equipment. Bad audio cannot be fixed in post. Bad video can be color-corrected, stabilized, and styled.

**Resolution matters less than people think.** A well-lit iPhone clip at 1080p with good audio is more usable than a 4K clip in a dark room with echo.

**Imperfection is a feature.** The slightly shaky, slightly imperfect footage from a participant-filmed session carries authenticity that crew-produced footage cannot replicate. Do not over-polish in post.

---

## Post-Production with Shipped Footage

When editing footage that was self-filmed by participants:

- **Be generous with what you keep.** The participant chose to film this. Honor their choices.
- **Don't over-correct color.** The natural lighting of their space is part of the story.
- **Preserve natural pauses.** Participants who filmed alone often have longer pauses — these are contemplative, not dead air.
- **Subtitle when necessary.** Self-filmed audio is sometimes uneven. Subtitles maintain accessibility without sacrificing authenticity.
- **Show the participant the edit before finalizing.** They are the author. They have final cut on their own story.
