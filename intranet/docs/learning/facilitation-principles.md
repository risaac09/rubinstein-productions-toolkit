# Facilitation Principles

These principles govern every facilitated interaction — whether a Mirror Session, a group workshop, or a casual conversation with a prospect. They are the methodological core of RP.

---

## The Five Core Principles

### 1. Facilitation Before Technology

Prioritize relational safety and nervous-system attunement before any camera setup, recording, or formal process begins. The technology serves the facilitation, never the reverse.

In practice: arrive early, set up equipment invisibly, establish the relational container through conversation before turning anything on.

### 2. Reciprocity Over Extraction

Design every interaction as a mutual exchange. Filming should leave both parties more coherent, not just the facilitator richer in material. The participant should feel they received something (clarity, reflection, being witnessed) — not that something was taken from them.

In practice: always deliver a Mirror Narrative or synthesis back to the participant. Never film without a clear plan to return value.

### 3. Psychological Safety as Production Asset

This is not a soft principle — it is a technical one. Emotional regulation directly improves narrative coherence and on-camera authenticity. A dysregulated participant produces defensive, rehearsed, useless footage. A regulated participant produces truth.

In practice: invest 30–40% of session time in Container work. It feels slow. It is the fastest path to authentic content.

### 4. Visible Consent Loops

Consent is ongoing, revisitable, and embodied — not a one-time signature on a release form. Check in throughout: "Are you still comfortable? Do you want to keep going? Is there anything you'd like to take off the table?"

In practice: check consent at minimum three times per session — beginning, midpoint, and before any sensitive territory. Read body language; don't wait for verbal cues.

### 5. Embodied Presence

The facilitator's own nervous-system state is part of the production environment. Your regulated body stabilizes the participant's system through co-regulation — vocal tone, facial expression, pacing, breathing.

In practice: do a 2–3 minute grounding practice before every session. Check: am I grounded, curious, non-performative? If the answer is no, address it before entering the room.

## The Facilitation Protocol

### Phase 0: Internal Regulation (Pre-Session)

- 2–3 minutes slow diaphragmatic breathing
- Internal check: Am I grounded, curious, non-performative?
- Set intention: "to witness truthfully" (not "to capture perfectly")
- Visualize participant's autonomy — they are author, not object

### Phase 1: Co-Regulation & Consent (First 15 Minutes)

- Arrange space for mutual visibility and comfort
- Keep gear visible, narrate setup
- Share purpose conversationally: "This isn't about performance. We're here to understand what feels most real for you."
- Invite boundaries: "Is there anything off-limits?"
- Ask permission to begin recording

### Phase 2: Facilitated Conversation (Core Session)

- Open with broad, invitational questions: "Tell me about..." / "What's alive for you right now?"
- Follow the energy, not a script
- Use silence generously — don't fill pauses
- Reflect back what you hear: "What I'm noticing is..." / "It sounds like..."
- When emotion surfaces, don't redirect — hold space
- When language shifts from borrowed to natural, lean in — that's the real material

### Phase 3: Integration & Close (Final 15 Minutes)

- Signal the wind-down: "We're coming toward the end. Is there anything you haven't said that wants to be said?"
- Reaffirm consent: "Is there anything from today you'd like to keep off the record?"
- Name what you observed (briefly): "What struck me was..."
- Explain next steps: when they'll receive the Mirror Narrative, what to expect
- Thank them specifically (not generically)

## Common Facilitator Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | What To Do Instead |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Rushing to content | Session feels "short" | Trust the Container. 30 minutes of safety produces 60 minutes of truth. |
| Filling silence | Discomfort with pauses | Count to 10 before responding. The participant is processing. |
| Asking leading questions | Wanting specific material | Ask open questions. "What happened?" not "Was it hard?" |
| Over-sharing your own experience | Building rapport | Brief mirroring is fine. Extended sharing shifts the center of gravity. |
| Diagnosing | Wanting to help | Observe and reflect. "I notice..." not "I think the problem is..." |
| Performing competence | Insecurity | The most competent facilitator is the one who listens most. |

## Group Facilitation Adaptations

When facilitating groups (workshops, team sessions):

- Container establishment takes longer — budget 20–30% of total time
- Establish explicit ground rules (confidentiality, consent, right to pass)
- Use rounds (everyone speaks) before opening to free discussion
- Watch for dominant voices and gently create space for quieter participants
- Pair or small-group breakouts preserve intimacy in larger settings
- Close with a round: one word or one sentence from each person
