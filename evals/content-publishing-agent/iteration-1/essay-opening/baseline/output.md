The grant officer closes her laptop halfway through the site visit and says, gently, "I want to make sure I understand — is this a youth workforce program, or a healing justice collective, or a policy shop? Because I've read three things this week from you, and they each sound like a different organization." The executive director laughs the laugh you laugh when something lands too close. Behind her, the program director is already drafting a response in her head about how of course they're all three, that's the point, that's the integrated model. The comms director, who wrote none of the three things, stares at the carpet.

I've watched some version of this scene play out in conference rooms, on Zoom calls, and in the back booths of diners from Providence to Oakland. A funder, a board member, a prospective hire, a journalist — someone from outside the organism — reflects back what the organization actually sounds like from the receiving end. And the people inside flinch, because they can hear it too, now that it's been named. The website says one thing. The annual report says another. The ED's LinkedIn posts say a third. The program team's Instagram says a fourth that nobody on the leadership team has actually read.

This isn't a branding problem, though it will get diagnosed as one and a consultant will get hired and a new visual identity will get rolled out and the problem will persist. This is a metabolism problem.
