The program officer is reading aloud from the org's website. The executive director is across the table, nodding, but her jaw is tight. Halfway through the "About" page, the ED interrupts: "That doesn't sound like us." The communications director, who wrote it eight months ago, goes quiet. The development director opens her laptop to pull up the case statement she sent funders last week — different language, different tone, a different theory of change implied in every sentence. Someone eventually says what everyone in the room has been feeling for an hour: "Are we even the same organization across these documents?"

I have been in some version of this room maybe forty times in the last three years. Health equity coalitions, public health nonprofits, advocacy orgs, clinical networks. The artifacts change — a website, a strategic plan, a pitch deck, an annual report, a grant narrative, a staff onboarding doc — but the diagnosis is the same. The organization is producing language faster than it is metabolizing meaning. What comes out the other end reads like three different organizations wearing the same logo.

I have started calling this an information metabolism problem, because I think the usual frames — "messaging inconsistency," "brand voice drift," "we need a comms refresh" — mistake a symptom for the condition. What I'm finding is that the sounds-like-three-organizations problem is almost never about writing. It is about how information moves through an org before it gets written down.
