I was filming a founder for her speaker reel last week. Between takes she pulled the lav off her collar and said, "I hate this. I hate hearing my own voice. I sound performative."

I said, "Yeah — because you're performing. Who are you talking TO in your head right now?"

She paused. "The person hiring me."

"Talk to your sister."

The next take was the one.

I keep noticing this. The camera doesn't make people performative. A misplaced audience does. When someone rehearses toward a gatekeeper — the hiring manager, the funder, the imagined judge — the body knows, and the voice goes thin.

The fix is rarely better delivery. It's a better listener. Pick someone real. Someone who already loves you. Aim the sentence at them and let the camera eavesdrop.

What I'm finding is that most "executive presence" work is actually an audience problem dressed up as a confidence problem. We train people to push harder toward the room that scares them, when what they need is permission to turn slightly — and speak to the person who already gets it.

Who are you talking to in your head when you record?

If the answer is the person deciding whether you're worthy, the take will sound like it.
