# Diagnosis: Why This Tagline Won't Stick

**Tagline under review:**
> "Rubinstein Productions empowers mission-driven organizations to amplify their authentic voice through innovative storytelling solutions."

## The Short Answer

This tagline is a Mad Libs of nonprofit-marketing clichés. Every noun and verb has been worn smooth by overuse. A reader could swap in any other agency name and the sentence would still be true — which means it identifies nothing, commits to nothing, and sticks to nothing.

## What's Wrong, Specifically

### 1. It fails the "swap test"
Replace "Rubinstein Productions" with "Acme Creative" or "GoodWorks Studio" and the sentence reads identically. A tagline that could belong to any of your competitors is not a tagline — it's wallpaper. Differentiation is the entire job of a tagline, and this one does none of it.

### 2. Every load-bearing word is a cliché
- **empowers** — meaningless business verb, used by everyone from enterprise SaaS to deodorant brands
- **mission-driven** — the default adjective for nonprofit work; signals nothing specific
- **amplify** — the most over-used verb in advocacy/comms marketing of the last decade
- **authentic voice** — so overused it now signals the opposite (inauthenticity)
- **innovative** — the word that proves you are not innovative; innovators show, they don't claim
- **storytelling solutions** — "solutions" is the tell of a business that can't name what it actually sells

Six buzzwords in 18 words. That's a buzzword every three words.

### 3. It's abstract where it should be concrete
There is no image, no friction, no specific person, no specific problem, no specific before/after. Who is the organization? What was broken? What changed? The Made to Stick "Concrete" principle says: if I can't picture it, I can't remember it. I cannot picture a single thing in this sentence.

### 4. It describes a category, not a point of view
"Storytelling for mission-driven organizations" is a *category* — dozens of firms occupy it. A sticky tagline stakes out a *position within* a category: a belief, a method, an enemy, a trade-off. This tagline has no opinion. It is not for or against anything.

### 5. It's written in corporate third-person
"Rubinstein Productions empowers..." is the voice of an About page, not a tagline. It is describing itself *to itself*. Sticky messaging speaks to the reader or from a stance ("We believe..." / "You deserve..." / "Stop..."). This one does neither.

### 6. It buries the actual value under abstraction
The real work Isaac does — facilitation, polyvagal co-regulation, "Say The Thing" methodology, helping leaders speak from their nervous system rather than their script — is nowhere in this sentence. The tagline abstracts away everything unusual and specific about the practice in favor of language that could describe a press-release distribution service.

### 7. It fails every SUCCESs criterion
- **Simple** — 18 words, no core idea I can extract
- **Unexpected** — zero surprise; it's what I'd predict before reading it
- **Concrete** — no image, no sensory detail, no specific case
- **Credible** — self-proclamation ("innovative," "authentic") destroys credibility
- **Emotional** — no feeling; reads like a grant-application boilerplate
- **Story** — no protagonist, no stakes, no change

## The Diagnostic Summary

This tagline is what a message looks like when someone has tried to describe their work *safely* to *everyone*. It is risk-averse. It is committee-approved. It is category-generic. It is the default output of the nonprofit-comms language model running in everyone's head.

To make it stick, it needs to do the opposite of what it's currently doing: get specific, take a position, name a real person or moment, use language no competitor would use, and drop every word that ends in "-solutions," "-driven," or "-ative."

The work isn't to polish this sentence. The work is to throw it out and start from a concrete story of one client, one moment, one transformation — and let the tagline fall out of that.
