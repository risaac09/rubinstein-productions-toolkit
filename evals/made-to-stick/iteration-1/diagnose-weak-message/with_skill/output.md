# Why This Tagline Won't Stick

**Tagline under review:**
> "Rubinstein Productions empowers mission-driven organizations to amplify their authentic voice through innovative storytelling solutions."

## Overall Score: 11/60 — Won't Stick (fundamental rework needed)

This is a textbook example of a message strangled by the Curse of Knowledge. It's the kind of sentence you'd find on any one of 10,000 agency websites — which is exactly the problem. Let me walk through each SUCCESs principle.

---

| Principle | Score | Diagnosis |

## Principle-by-Principle Breakdown

### 1. Simple — 3/10

The tagline tries to say four things at once:
1. We empower organizations
2. Those orgs are mission-driven
3. We amplify their authentic voice
4. Via innovative storytelling solutions

**Commander's Intent fails.** If someone only remembered ONE thing, what would it be? There isn't one. It's a soup of claims.

**Test:** Could a 12-year-old repeat this back? No. Could a fellow filmmaker? Probably not verbatim either. The sentence structure is so nested ("empowers X to do Y through Z") that the core idea dissolves.

**Compare Southwest's "THE low-fare airline"** — three words, one promise, zero ambiguity. This tagline has 15 words and five promises.

### 2. Unexpected — 1/10

Swap "Rubinstein Productions" with "[Any Agency Name]" and the sentence works identically well for a branding firm in Des Moines, a nonprofit consultancy in Oakland, or a video company in Berlin. That's the diagnostic test — **a tagline that fits every competitor is a tagline for nobody.**

Every word here is expected:
- "empowers" — expected
- "mission-driven" — expected (and becoming a cliché)
- "amplify" — expected
- "authentic voice" — expected
- "innovative storytelling solutions" — deeply expected

There is no surprise, no pattern-break, no curiosity gap. A prospect's eyes glaze before they finish reading.

### 3. Concrete — 1/10

This is the biggest failure. Count the abstractions: **empower, mission-driven, amplify, authentic, voice, innovative, storytelling, solutions.** Eight abstract nouns/verbs in one sentence. Zero sensory detail.

**What can a reader picture?** Nothing. There's no customer, no room, no camera, no transformation, no outcome. "Voice" here isn't even concrete voice — it's metaphorical voice. "Solutions" is the emptiest word in B2B marketing.

**Apply the Velcro test:** What memory-hook does this sentence offer? None. It slides off the brain.

**Concrete alternatives might look like:**
- "We help nonprofits raise $2M more by sounding like humans, not brochures."
- "We make the films that get you funded."
- "30-minute videos that double your donor base."

(Numbers are illustrative — the point is that concrete specifics create pictures.)

### 4. Credible — 2/10

Why should anyone believe this claim? The tagline offers zero proof:
- No Sinatra Test (no "we made the film for X" that would prove the rest)
- No testable credential
- No specific client, outcome, or number
- No "anti-authority" (no real customer voice)
- "Innovative" is a credibility-destroying word — if you were truly innovative, you'd describe the innovation instead of asserting it

Saying "innovative storytelling solutions" is like saying "I am funny." The only people who need to assert innovation are those who can't demonstrate it.

### 5. Emotional — 2/10

The Mother Teresa principle says: if I look at the one, I will act. This tagline has no "one." There's no person, no pain, no desire, no stakes.

Worse, it addresses organizations, not humans. Organizations don't buy anything — people at organizations do. An executive director reading this feels nothing because the tagline never says "you" or describes what she actually feels at 11pm on a Tuesday when her annual report still isn't written and her board meets Friday.

**No identity frame either.** Who does a mission-driven leader become by hiring Rubinstein Productions? The sentence doesn't say. By contrast, "Don't Mess with Texas" worked because it told Texans who they were.

### 6. Stories — 2/10

Zero narrative. No character, no problem, no journey, no outcome. Just a flat assertion of capability.

Where's the customer who was invisible until their story got told? Where's the founder whose grant application kept getting rejected until they stopped sounding like a brochure? Those stories exist in Isaac's actual work — none of them are in this tagline.

---

## The Curse of Knowledge at Work

This tagline reads like it was written by someone who already knows:
- What Rubinstein Productions actually does
- What "Say Why" means as a methodology
- What distinguishes this shop from 10,000 others
- Why "authentic voice" matters in Isaac's specific practice

None of that knowledge is transmitted to an outsider. The tagline assumes the reader will fill in meaning the writer already has. They won't.

**The writer knows the "voice liberation" lineage — polyvagal co-regulation, relational ontology, "Say The Thing" methodology.** None of that density comes through. The tagline sounds like a generic agency because all the specificity has been abstracted out in the name of sounding "professional."

---

## The Core Problem

**This is a category tagline, not a Rubinstein Productions tagline.**

It describes what *a creative agency serving nonprofits* does, not what *Isaac* specifically does. The tagline is interchangeable with competitors, which means it does no positioning work, no differentiation work, and no memory work.

---

## What a Sticky Version Would Need

To reach 50+/60, a replacement tagline should:

1. **Simple:** ONE promise, under 10 words. Commander's Intent: "If they remember one thing, what is it?"
2. **Unexpected:** Break a pattern. What do *bad* nonprofit videos do that Rubinstein refuses to do? Lead with that negation.
3. **Concrete:** A number, a sensory detail, or a specific outcome. "Raise $X," "in 30 days," "without sounding like a brochure."
4. **Credible:** Either a Sinatra Test client, a specific proof point, or an anti-authority (real customer language).
5. **Emotional:** Address one person's felt problem — the ED staring at a blank grant doc, the founder whose story keeps getting mangled.
6. **Stories:** At minimum, imply a before/after. "From invisible to funded." "From jargon to yes."

**Directional examples** (not finished taglines, just demonstrations of the register):
- "We make the films that win grants."
- "Nonprofit videos that don't sound like nonprofits."
- "From mission statement to moving story — in one shoot."
- "The film crew that asks better questions than your therapist."

Each of these is concrete, breaks a pattern, implies a story, and couldn't be swapped onto a competitor's website without sounding wrong.

---

## Recommended Next Step

Before writing a replacement, do the **Commander's Intent exercise**:

> If a past client had to describe Rubinstein Productions in one sentence to a friend, what would they actually say?

That sentence — the real one, from a real mouth — is where the sticky tagline lives. The current tagline is what the website says. The sticky version is what the client says at a dinner party.
