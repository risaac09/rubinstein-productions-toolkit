# Diagnosing 2-Month SaaS Churn

Your client has been treating symptoms, not causes. Price, emails, and referrals are acquisition/retention *levers*, but they don't tell you *why* people leave. Before pulling more levers, you need to diagnose the actual problem.

## The core issue: they don't know why users are cancelling

Every retention fix they've tried is a guess. Lower pricing assumes cost is the objection. More emails assume users don't understand the product. A referral program assumes happy users will evangelize. None of these have worked, which suggests none of those assumptions are correct.

## Hypotheses to test

The 2-month timing is a strong signal. It usually means one of these:

**1. The product delivers initial value but no recurring value.**
Users sign up, solve a one-time problem, and leave. Common in tools that feel like projects, not habits. Test by looking at what users *do* in weeks 1-2 vs weeks 5-8. If usage drops off a cliff around week 3-4, they've extracted what they needed.

**2. There's a "value ceiling" users hit fast.**
The product works great for the first use case, but doesn't scale with the user's needs. They outgrow it or find it doesn't cover the adjacent problems.

**3. Onboarding creates a false sense of fit.**
Users get excited in month 1, then discover in month 2 that the product doesn't actually solve their deeper job. More onboarding emails make this *worse*, not better — they delay the disappointment.

**4. The job-to-be-done is episodic, not continuous.**
Some tools are only needed periodically (tax software, event planning, hiring tools). If users think in project terms and you're charging in subscription terms, 2 months is exactly when the mismatch surfaces.

**5. A competitor or free alternative becomes obvious around week 6-8.**
Users discover substitutes. This is especially true if the product is in a crowded category.

## What to actually do

**Stop guessing. Go talk to the people who left.**

Pull a list of the last 20-30 customers who cancelled at the 2-month mark. Interview 10-15 of them. Don't ask "why did you leave?" — that gets rationalizations. Instead ask:

- Walk me through the day you decided to cancel. What was happening?
- What were you trying to accomplish when you first signed up?
- What were you using before this? What are you using now?
- If I could have changed one thing to make you stay, what would it be?
- What would have to be true for you to come back?

Also look at the **usage data**: when did engagement actually stop? The cancellation date is a lagging indicator — people usually check out 2-4 weeks before they cancel. Find the real disengagement moment.

## Reframe the question

Your client is asking "how do we stop churn?" The better question is: **"What job did users hire us for, and are we still doing that job in month 2?"**

If the answer is no, no amount of pricing, email, or referral tactics will fix it. If the answer is yes, you have a communication/engagement problem, not a product problem — and you'll know which levers to pull.

## Immediate next step

Before recommending anything else, get the client to commit to 10 cancelled-customer interviews in the next 2 weeks. The patterns will show up fast, and you'll have directional evidence instead of more guesses.
