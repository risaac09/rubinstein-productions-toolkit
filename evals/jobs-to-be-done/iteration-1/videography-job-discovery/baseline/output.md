# What Job Is a Nonprofit ED Hiring a Videographer To Do?

## The Surface Job (what the RFP says)

"Produce a 2-3 minute brand video that tells our story."

This is the functional deliverable. It's real, but it's not what you're actually being paid for. If it were, any competent camera operator at half your rate would suffice.

## The Real Jobs (what the ED is actually hiring)

A nonprofit Executive Director commissioning a brand video is almost never hiring "a video." They are hiring a video to do a job — usually several stacked jobs at once. Sorted roughly from functional → emotional → social:

### 1. Functional jobs

- **Make the case for support in a room I can't be in.** The video is a proxy for the ED at galas, board meetings, major-donor cultivation dinners, foundation site visits, and grant submissions. It does persuasion work while she sleeps.
- **Compress ten years of complexity into 3 minutes a stranger can grasp.** Translate programs, theory of change, and impact into something a distracted prospect understands on first viewing.
- **Produce an artifact I can reuse across channels for 2-3 years.** One investment, many deployments: website hero, email campaigns, annual report, board onboarding, social cutdowns.
- **Give my development team a tool that shortens the fundraising cycle.** The video is supposed to move a "maybe" donor to "yes" faster, or to raise the average gift size.

### 2. Emotional jobs (the ones she won't say out loud)

- **Make me feel proud of the organization I lead.** EDs are often burned out, isolated, and uncertain whether the work is landing. A good brand video reflects the org back to her in a way that reminds her why she's doing this. It's partly a mirror for the leader.
- **Relieve the anxiety that our story is being told badly (or not at all).** She's been watching peer orgs produce slicker media and fearing donor attention is drifting. Hiring you quiets that specific fear.
- **Let me stop explaining.** EDs are exhausted from repeating the mission in every meeting. The video offloads the explaining job so she can do higher-leverage work.
- **Make the people we serve feel seen, not extracted from.** She's haunted by poverty-porn nonprofit video tropes. She's hiring you partly to NOT produce that — to handle participants with dignity. This is often the deepest unspoken criterion.
- **Give staff and participants something that makes them feel valued.** The production process itself is part of the deliverable. Being filmed well is a form of being honored.

### 3. Social / identity jobs

- **Signal to funders that we're a serious, modern organization.** Production value is a proxy for operational competence. A polished video tells a program officer "these people can execute."
- **Position me as an ED who invests in communications.** Internally (board) and externally (sector peers), commissioning quality media is a leadership move. It says: we're not scrappy-by-default, we're strategic.
- **Give the board something to be excited about at the next meeting.** The video is a deliverable the ED can show the board as evidence of momentum.
- **Help us recruit — staff, volunteers, partners.** Increasingly brand videos are used to attract talent and partners, not just donors.

## What this means you're actually being paid for

You're being paid to be a **trusted narrative steward during a vulnerable moment in the org's life**. The ED is handing you:

- Her people (to be filmed with care)
- Her story (to be told without distortion)
- Her reputation (staked on what you produce)
- Her scarce budget (often one of the larger discretionary line items of the year)

The "video" is the artifact. The **job** is: *reduce the ED's anxiety that her org's story is being told with dignity, strategic clarity, and enough production value to move money and attention — and do it in a process that honors her staff and participants rather than extracting from them.*

## The competing alternatives (what she's firing to hire you)

Understanding the job means understanding what she's choosing you *over*:

- A cheaper videographer (fires: dignity, strategic fit, nervous-system safety on set)
- A volunteer with a camera (fires: production value, reliability, the "signal to funders" job)
- DIY phone content (fires: the "I can stop explaining" job, the reusability job)
- Doing nothing / waiting another year (fires: the peer-anxiety job, the board-momentum job)
- A marketing agency package (fires: the craft/dignity job, usually; fires price)

If you know which alternative she was leaning toward before you, you know which job she's actually paying you to do.

## Practical implication for pricing and positioning

If you price "video production" you compete on day rates. If you price **"narrative stewardship that moves money, honors participants, and lets the ED stop explaining,"** you compete on trust and outcomes — and the reference price is the fundraising lift or the avoided cost of a botched story, not the camera day.

Your real deliverable is a **peace-of-mind object** wrapped around a video file. Charge for the peace of mind.

## The one question to ask in the next discovery call

> "Imagine the video is finished and it's working perfectly. What has changed for you, for your team, and for the people you serve?"

Her answer names the job. Everything else is spec.
