SUBJECT: The translation problem you named

Jennifer,

I've been sitting with something you said on Tuesday, and I wanted to come back to it before suggesting any next steps.

You said the board keeps asking for a "refreshed message," but that you don't think Threshold has a message problem — you have a translation problem. Between what clinicians can say in a group room, where stigma and liability fall away because the container holds them, and what you can safely put on a website, where every sentence gets read by someone in crisis, a referring provider, a funder, a compliance reviewer, and possibly a plaintiff's attorney, all at once.

That's a real distinction, and I think it's the more honest frame. A "refresh" implies the words are stale. What you're actually describing is that the truest language — the language that happens in group, the language clients actually use when they're metabolizing what's happening to them — can't survive the trip to the website intact. It gets flattened by the audiences it has to serve simultaneously. So what ends up on the site isn't wrong, exactly. It's just translated down to the floor of what every audience can tolerate, which means it loses the specific thing that makes clinical work at Threshold feel different from clinical work anywhere else.

A few things I'm curious about, that I don't have answers to yet:

One — when clinicians describe the work to a new client in an intake, what do they actually say? Not the script. The real sentences. Because that's usually where the untranslated version lives, and it's often closer to website-ready than people assume, once you understand which specific words are carrying the liability weight and which ones are just carrying old assumptions about what's "professional."

Two — who is actually reading the website, in what order, and what are they each trying to decide? A family member at 2am deciding whether to call is making a different decision than a referring clinician vetting you for their caseload. The translation problem might not be one problem. It might be three or four problems wearing the same coat, and some of them are more solvable than others.

Three — what specifically has the board flagged as feeling stale? Sometimes "refresh the message" is code for something else entirely — a competitor showed up, a funder gave feedback, someone on the board read a marketing book. Knowing what triggered the ask changes what "done" looks like.

I don't want to pitch you a scope before I understand the shape of this better. If it'd be useful, I could sit in on one group session (with full client consent, obviously, and only if your clinical team thinks it's appropriate), and separately listen to how two or three clinicians describe the work in their own words. That would give me the raw material to see where the translation is actually breaking down — not guess at it.

No rush on this. I know you're juggling the board ask alongside actual clinical load. Let me know if the frame lands, and if the listening approach feels right, we can figure out timing.
