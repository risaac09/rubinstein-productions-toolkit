# Reconnecting with Sam Levine — Thinking It Through

## The shame is the signal, not the obstacle

The shame you're feeling is doing something useful: it's telling you this relationship mattered. Transactional acquaintances don't generate shame when they lapse. You feel it because Sam was a real node in your life, and the drift feels like a small betrayal of that closeness — both of what he gave you (the client intro) and of the friendship itself.

But shame also distorts. It makes the gap feel bigger than it probably is to him. It makes you pre-rehearse his judgment. It tempts you toward either (a) over-explaining / apologizing your way back in, or (b) defaulting to a light, breezy "hey long time!" that papers over the realness. Both are avoidance costumes.

The move is to name the gap honestly, once, and then be present — not to perform contrition, and not to pretend no time passed.

## What genuine reconnection actually looks like

Genuine reconnection has a few features:

1. **It doesn't lead with a request.** Not a coffee ask, not a "pick your brain," not a referral. The reach-out itself is the whole thing.
2. **It acknowledges the gap without drowning in it.** One sentence. No elaborate apology. Shame-as-preamble makes the other person do emotional labor to reassure you.
3. **It carries specificity.** It shows you've been thinking about *him* — not just noticed a gap on your side. A memory, a thing he said that stuck, a moment you're grateful for, a thing you noticed he's doing now.
4. **It offers presence, not transaction.** "I'd love to hear how you are" — and mean it. Not "let's catch up and also here's what I'm working on."
5. **It leaves room for him.** No pressure to respond on a timeline. No assumed yes.

## What to think about before writing anything

Some questions worth sitting with:

- **What did Sam actually do for you?** Not just the client intro — what was the texture of that friendship? What did you talk about? What did he bring out in you?
- **What do you know about his transition?** If you know he left a job and started consulting, you probably have some sense of *why* (burnout? values shift? opportunity?). Reaching out to someone in a career pivot lands very differently than reaching out to someone whose life looks identical to before. He may be in a tender moment. He may be hungry for people who knew him before the transition.
- **What, honestly, are you hoping for?** A rebuilt friendship? An occasional-but-real thread? Just closure on the guilt? All are legitimate — but know which one, so you don't accidentally promise more than you want to give.
- **Is there any actual grudge or hurt underneath the drift?** Sometimes "life got busy" is partly true and partly a euphemism. Worth checking.

## The shape of a first message

Not a template — a shape:

- Open with him, not with you. ("Been thinking about you" / "Saw [thing] and thought of you.")
- Name the gap cleanly. One sentence. ("It's been too long and that's on me as much as anyone.")
- Acknowledge what he gave you, once. The client intro changed your trajectory — he should know you know that. But don't make the whole message a gratitude monologue; that can feel like payoff-now-goodbye energy.
- Ask about him, specifically. The consulting transition is a natural door. "How's the consulting life treating you?" is better than "how are you?" because it shows you know what's going on.
- Close low-stakes. "No pressure to write back — just wanted you on my radar again." Or propose one small thing: a walk, a call, coffee if he's local.

## What to avoid

- **The apology spiral.** "I'm so sorry it's been so long, I've been a terrible friend, I should have…" — makes him comfort you.
- **The highlight reel.** Don't lead with what you've been up to. That reads as "here's my update, respond in kind." Save your updates for when he asks.
- **The immediate ask.** Even if you have a legit reason to reconnect (referral, collab, advice), don't put it in the first message. Rebuild the thread first.
- **Performative vulnerability.** "I've been doing so much inner work on why I let relationships drift…" — save that for when you're actually in conversation with him.
- **Generic warmth.** "Hope you're well!" is what you write to people whose last name you're not sure of. Sam deserves more texture.

## A quieter reframe

Two years is a long time, but it's not unrecoverable — especially with someone who showed up for you once already. People who introduce you to your first big client tend to be the kind of people who understand that friendships ebb. He may have been thinking about you too, and feeling the same slight weight about reaching out. You reaching out first dissolves that for both of you.

The shame you feel about the gap is the same muscle that's going to make the reconnection real. Don't try to skip past it — let it inform a message that has some weight to it. Just don't let it *be* the message.

## One concrete next step

Before drafting anything, spend 10 minutes writing (just for yourself, not to send):
- Three specific things you remember about Sam or your friendship.
- One thing you're genuinely curious about in his life right now.
- What you actually hope this reconnection becomes, realistically, over the next year.

Then draft from that. The message will write itself, and it won't sound like a LinkedIn reconnect.
