# Reconnecting with Sam Levine — A Thinking-Through

*Run through network-stewardship skill | April 2026*

---

## First: the shame

Before anything tactical, let's name what's actually happening. You said "I feel some shame about how long it's been." That shame is doing something — it's the reason you haven't reached out yet, and if you don't address it, it will leak into whatever message you send. Shame-driven outreach reads as apology-performance, which is its own kind of transactional move. The other person has to reassure you instead of just... being reached out to.

**The reframe:** Two years of silence between friends who mattered to each other is not a moral failing. It's ordinary life. Sam has also been silent for two years. He is not sitting there tallying your absence — he's been in a career transition, which is disorienting and consuming. If you carry the shame into the message, you're asking him to carry it with you. Drop it before you write.

One somatic cue: if drafting feels like an apology letter, you're still in shame. If it feels like picking up a conversation, you're clear.

---

## Why now? (the protocol question)

The skill asks: what's the *genuine* reason? Not "it's been a while" — that's a calendar observation, not a reason. Walk through the real candidates:

1. **He came to mind.** Something surfaced him for you recently enough that you're writing this. What was it? A project that reminded you of a conversation you two had? Someone mentioning him? A memory of the intro he made to your first big client? Whatever it was — *that* is the "why now." Lead with it.

2. **The career transition.** He left his job and started consulting. That's a liminal passage — you know this territory. You run a consultancy. You know what the first year is like. That's not a reason to pitch help; it's a reason to witness him. "How's it actually going?" from someone who's walked that road is different from the generic check-in he's probably received plenty of.

3. **You owe him an acknowledgment.** Not a thank-you-for-the-client (that was two years ago and he's heard it). But a specific one: "That intro to [client name] ended up shaping the last two years of my work in ways I didn't see at the time. I want to tell you what came of it."

Option 3 is probably the strongest opening — it's specific, it's gratitude (per the skill's weekly gratitude practice: specific, not "thanks for everything"), and it gives Sam a small gift: the knowledge that something he did had downstream meaning.

---

## What's the offer?

The skill asks: can you bring something? The offer here isn't a service or a referral. The offer is **attention and memory**. You remember what he did. You're returning something to him — the story of what grew from his gesture. For someone one year into consulting, that kind of downstream proof matters. It's the feedback loop the new consultant rarely gets.

If the conversation opens up and he's in the thick of building his practice, *then* you might have things to offer — intros, lessons learned, the "here's what I wish I'd known year one" conversation. But don't lead with offers of help. That's presumptuous and it pre-assigns roles (you = stable, him = struggling).

---

## What's the CTA?

Three options, ranked:

1. **No CTA.** Just the gratitude note. No ask, no coffee request, no "let's catch up." This is the cleanest play if you want to test the field without pressure. He'll respond if he wants to; if he doesn't, you've still given him something real.

2. **Soft invitation to a call** — "If you ever want to compare notes on the first year of consulting, I'd love to." Leaves it in his court entirely. No proposed time. No urgency.

3. **Direct coffee/call request with a specific window.** Only use this if you're genuinely certain you want the sustained reconnection. If the shame is still in the driver's seat, the direct ask will feel like you're trying to clear a debt.

Given the two-year gap, I'd start with **Option 1 or 2**. Let him meet you in the opening. The relationship re-forms on his timing as much as yours.

---

## Sacral check

Before you send: does this feel alive, or does it feel like closing a loop out of guilt? If the latter, don't send. Wait until he actually comes to mind for a reason that isn't "I should have reached out by now." Relationships sense performance. The two-year gap is fine — another month is also fine.

## A possible shape (not a draft — that's outreach-email-manager's job)

If you wrote it today, the structure would be:

1. Specific memory or gratitude — what he did and what came of it
2. Honest acknowledgment of the gap without over-apologizing ("it's been a while — I've been thinking about you lately because [real reason]")
3. Genuine curiosity about where he is now in the transition
4. Soft or no CTA — leave him room

What you're avoiding: "Hey, long time, we should catch up" (generic), "I'm so sorry I've been bad at staying in touch" (shame-forward), "I'd love to pick your brain about X" (extractive), or any framing that treats the gap as the subject. The subject is *him*, and what he gave you, and what you're curious about in his life now.
