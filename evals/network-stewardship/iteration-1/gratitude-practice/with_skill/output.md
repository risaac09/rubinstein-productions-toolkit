# Q1 Gratitude Messages — Three People

Per the network-stewardship skill: gratitude is specific, not transactional, does not require a response, and names what was actually given. No template adapted three ways — three different relationships, three different registers.

---

## 1. Rachel (sister) — Tier 1, Core Circle

**Channel:** text message or voice memo (sister, not email)
**Register:** intimate, unpolished, no professional framing

> Rach — I've been meaning to say this for a minute and kept almost-saying-it and not. Q1 was harder than I let on in real time, and you knew. You didn't try to fix any of it. You just kept picking up when I called and let me be the mess I actually was, not the version I usually present. The thing you said in February about how I was "performing fine" — I've thought about that probably thirty times since. It broke something open I needed broken. I don't take it for granted that I get to be a whole person with you. Love you.

**Why this works per skill:** Core Circle = "show up fully when present." This isn't a check-in — it's naming what she gave specifically (letting him be unperformed) and referencing one concrete moment. No CTA. No request. Sacral-authentic.

---

## 2. Rob Chen (business mentor) — Tier 2, Strategic Ally

**Channel:** email
**Register:** professional warmth, specific, peer-adjacent (not student-to-guru)

**Subject:** The pricing conversation — an update

Rob —

Short one. I wanted to close a loop from our January call.

You told me to raise my prices and I was scared to. I did it anyway — not all the way to where you pushed me, but meaningfully up. Two things I didn't expect:

One, nobody balked. The clients who were going to say yes said yes at the new number. Nothing about the relationship changed except I'm no longer resentful at the end of a project.

Two, I started screening differently at the top of the funnel. The price increase did work I thought only discipline could do — it pre-qualified people.

I'm writing because I want you to know the advice landed and what it produced. You've given me a lot of this over the years and I don't always come back around to tell you it worked. This one did.

No ask here. Just gratitude.

Isaac

**Why this works per skill:** Tier 2 = "personalized outreach, genuine interest, offer before asking." The offer here IS the update — mentors rarely hear whether their advice landed. Specific (the January call, the pricing number, the two unexpected effects). Explicit "no ask." Closes a loop.

## 3. Jennifer Wells (client who referred two prospects unprompted) — Tier 2 → moving toward Strategic Ally

**Channel:** email, possibly followed by a small physical gesture
**Register:** professional, warm, acknowledges the specific act without making it weird

**Subject:** Thank you — and I noticed

Jennifer —

I want to say something directly rather than let it sit implied.

You sent two people my way this quarter without me ever asking, and both conversations have been substantive. I know referrals aren't free — you're putting your own judgment on the line when you send someone toward me. That's not a thing I take casually.

I've been thinking about what it means that you've chosen to do that. My read is that the work we did together actually changed something for you, and you're telling people about it not because I asked you to but because it felt true to. That's the referral I care about most — the kind that comes from real use, not obligation.

So — thank you. Genuinely. I'm going to keep earning it.

If there's ever anything I can amplify on your end, introductions I can make, or work you're circling that I might be useful to, I'd welcome the chance to return the energy.

Warmly,
Isaac

**Why this works per skill:** Tier 2 with referral-source = "offer before asking" AND "giving/receiving roughly balanced." Names the specific act (two referrals, unprompted), names what it signals (real use, not obligation), offers reciprocity without making it a quid-pro-quo. The "I'm going to keep earning it" closes without being sycophantic. The offer at the end is sincere and open-ended — not a pitch.
