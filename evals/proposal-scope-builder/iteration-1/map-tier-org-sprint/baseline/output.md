## The situation as I understand it

Delta Housing Coalition is doing serious work — 12 staff, a $2.1M budget, a mission with real stakes for the region's affordable-housing landscape. But the organization is speaking in three different voices. The website introduces one organization. Grant narratives describe a second. Tenant-facing materials sound like a third.

This is not a design problem. A new color palette and typeface will not solve it. What you're describing is a narrative fragmentation problem — the organization has never excavated and named its actual story, so each channel has drifted toward the conventions of its audience. Funders get foundation-speak. Tenants get service-speak. The public gets mission-statement-speak. None of them are wrong, exactly. But together they don't add up to a coherent organization.

Your instinct — that narrative excavation must come first, then voice consistency across channels — is correct. That's the work I do. Most "brand refresh" vendors would start with visual identity and bolt voice on at the end. That sequence produces the problem you already have.

## What the Map tier delivers

The Map tier is a focused 3-5 day sprint designed to surface the organization's true narrative — the story underneath the story — and produce a working document that every future channel can be built from. It is not a full brand system. It is the foundation that makes a brand system possible.

By the end of the sprint, Delta Housing Coalition will have:

1. **A narrative core document** — the organization's actual story, excavated from interviews with staff, board, and (where appropriate) tenants. This names what Delta Housing Coalition is *for*, who it serves, what it believes, and what makes it different from every other affordable-housing organization in the region.

2. **A voice architecture** — three to five voice principles with concrete do/don't examples, written to be usable by any staff member drafting copy. Not abstract adjectives ("warm, professional, bold") but operational guidance any writer on your team can apply in real time.

3. **A channel audit + reconciliation map** — a side-by-side look at how the website, grant narratives, and tenant-facing materials currently diverge, with a specific reconciliation path for each channel. This is the document Reina and her team will use to rewrite existing materials over the following months.

4. **A one-page narrative summary** — the story distilled to a single page, usable for board orientation, new-hire onboarding, and funder conversations.

## What this does NOT include

Being direct about scope so we're aligned:

- This is not a visual brand system (logo, color, typography). The narrative work comes first; visual identity is a separate engagement — ideally commissioned AFTER the narrative core is settled.
- This does not include rewriting the website, grant narratives, or tenant materials. It produces the map Reina's team will use to rewrite them, on your own timeline.
- This does not include implementation support beyond the final working session. If you want me to stay engaged through implementation (coaching, copy review, workshop facilitation for staff), that's a separate conversation — and something we could scope after Map wraps.

## Timing fit with your 4-month deadline

Your fiscal year end is roughly 4 months out (late July / early August 2026). Here's how Map sets you up:

- **Weeks 1-3 (April):** Map sprint completes
- **Weeks 4-10 (May-June):** Reina's team uses the reconciliation map to rewrite priority materials (website, next grant cycle, core tenant comms)
- **Weeks 11-14 (July):** Visual identity work (separate vendor or expanded engagement) can layer cleanly on top of settled narrative
- **Fiscal year end:** Coherent voice across channels, with the visual refresh as the last layer rather than the first

If you try to do visual refresh first in a 4-month window, you will end up with a prettier version of the three-voices problem. If you do narrative first, the visual work becomes straightforward.
