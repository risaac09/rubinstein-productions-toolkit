# Critique: 5 Client Interview Questions

## Overall Assessment

Most of these questions are poorly designed for customer discovery. They are leading, hypothetical, compliment-fishing, or ask for opinions rather than facts. The goal of a past-client interview is to understand concrete behavior, decisions, and context — not to collect flattery or speculation. Below is a rating of each question plus a replacement.

---

## Question-by-Question Ratings

### Q1: "Would you recommend working with me?"
**Rating: 1/5 — Bad (compliment-fishing / yes-no leading)**

This is a yes/no question that invites politeness. Almost everyone says "yes" to someone's face, especially a past vendor they had a reasonable experience with. It produces zero actionable signal about *why* they hired you or what drove the decision. It also reveals your emotional stake, which biases the rest of the interview.

**Replace with:** "Walk me through the last time you actually referred me (or almost referred me) to someone. What was happening, and what did you say?"
— This asks about concrete past behavior. If they've never referred you, that itself is a data point.

---

### Q2: "What did you like best about the final video?"
**Rating: 2/5 — Weak (compliment-fishing, wrong target)**

This asks for opinions about the deliverable, not about the decision to hire you. Clients will offer polite praise. You're interviewing to learn what made them *hire* you — the purchasing decision, the alternatives they considered, the job they needed done. What they liked about the video is a delivery-quality question, not a hiring-motivation question.

**Replace with:** "Take me back to when you first decided you needed a video. What was going on in the business that week? What had you tried before deciding to hire someone?"
— This surfaces the triggering event and the competing solutions they rejected.

---

### Q3: "Do you think founders need more storytelling help these days?"
**Rating: 0/5 — Terrible (leading, hypothetical, opinion-fishing)**

This is the worst question on the list. It's leading ("need more... these days" primes a yes), hypothetical (asking them to speculate about a category), and asks for opinions about other people's needs. It's also transparently pitching your thesis. Clients will nod along to be supportive. You'll learn nothing.

**Replace with:** "When you were trying to tell your founder story before we worked together, what did you actually try? What broke down?"
— Concrete, about their own past behavior, reveals whether storytelling was even the real job.

---

### Q4: "If we worked together again, what would you want me to do differently?"
**Rating: 3/5 — Okay but hypothetical**

This is the strongest question of the five, but it's still hypothetical ("if we worked together again") and invites polite, vague answers like "nothing, it was great!" It also doesn't anchor to a specific moment where friction actually occurred.

**Replace with:** "Tell me about a moment during our project where you were frustrated, confused, or had to chase me down. What happened?"
— This anchors to a real past event and gives permission to surface friction.

---

### Q5: "How important is narrative quality to you?"
**Rating: 1/5 — Bad (abstract, leading, self-reported importance)**

Asking someone to rate the importance of something in the abstract is near-worthless. Everyone says "very important" when asked directly — especially when the person asking clearly values it. Self-reported importance rarely matches revealed behavior (what they actually pay for, spend time on, or fight for internally).

**Replace with:** "When you showed the final video to your team or investors, what did you point to first? What did *they* comment on?"
— Reveals what actually mattered downstream, through observed reactions rather than stated values.

## Which to Replace

**Replace all five, but prioritize Q3, Q1, and Q5 first** — they will actively mislead you. Q2 and Q4 can be salvaged with the rewrites above.

## Core principle

You're not trying to learn whether they liked the video. You're trying to learn:
1. What triggered them to start looking (the Job)
2. What alternatives they considered and rejected
3. What happened during the decision that tipped them toward you
4. What friction they experienced and didn't tell you about
5. What downstream reactions they observed from their own audience

Ask about the past, about specifics, about their life — not about your idea.
