# Mom Test Critique: Your 5 Planned Client Interview Questions

**Overall score of your current question set: 2/10**

These questions are friendly and easy to answer, which is exactly the problem. Most of them ask for opinions, compliments, or hypotheticals — the three categories of data Mom Test explicitly warns are worthless. To reach 10/10, every question needs to be anchored in specific past behavior, not feelings or future predictions.

Here's the breakdown.

---

## Question-by-Question Rating

### Q1: "Would you recommend working with me?"
**Score: 1/10 — REPLACE**

This is a compliment trap. It's a yes/no that only has one socially acceptable answer (yes — they already hired you, and saying no to your face is rude). It produces zero information about why they hired you, whether they'd actually refer, or what the real decision factors were. It fails Rule 1 (it's about you, not their life) and is a textbook fishing-for-compliments question.

**Replace with:** *"Who have you actually referred me to since we worked together, and what did you tell them?"*
This anchors to past behavior. If they've referred you, you'll learn the exact language they use to describe your value (gold for positioning). If they haven't, that silence is also real data — and much more honest than "yes I'd recommend you."

---

### Q2: "What did you like best about the final video?"
**Score: 3/10 — REPLACE**

Better than Q1 because it points at a concrete artifact, but it still asks for an opinion ("what did you like") rather than behavior. It's also leading — it presupposes they liked something, and compliments are what you'll get. You won't learn what made them *hire* you (your stated goal), which happened before the video even existed.

**Replace with:** *"Walk me through the moment you decided to hire me. What had you tried before, and what made you pull the trigger this time?"*
This gets at the actual hiring decision with past-tense specificity. You'll learn the real alternatives they considered, the trigger event, and the language of the problem they were trying to solve.

---

### Q3: "Do you think founders need more storytelling help these days?"
**Score: 0/10 — REPLACE**

Worst question in the set. It's:
- Hypothetical ("do you think")
- About a generic population ("founders"), not them
- Leading toward a "yes" that validates your business
- Completely disconnected from their actual life or behavior

The answer is always yes and tells you nothing. This is the canonical Mom Test failure.

**Replace with:** *"When was the last time you or a peer founder had something important to communicate and it didn't land the way you hoped? What happened?"*
Specific, past-tense, about their actual life, and has the power to surprise you. You might learn the real pain isn't storytelling at all — it's something adjacent you could serve better.

---

### Q4: "If we worked together again, what would you want me to do differently?"
**Score: 6/10 — KEEP WITH MODIFICATION**

This is your strongest question. It invites critical feedback and gives them permission to be honest. Still hypothetical though ("if we worked again"), and many clients will default to "nothing, it was great!" to be polite.

**Strengthen to:** *"Think back to a moment during our project when you felt frustrated or confused. What was happening, and what did you do about it?"*
This pulls a specific past moment of friction instead of asking them to invent future improvements. You'll get concrete process gaps, not polite suggestions.

---

### Q5: "How important is narrative quality to you?"
**Score: 1/10 — REPLACE**

Another opinion/rating question with an obvious socially correct answer ("very important"). It also leads with your framing ("narrative quality" is *your* language, not theirs). You'll learn nothing about what they actually value or what they'd pay for.

**Replace with:** *"What's your current videography budget line, and where does that money come from? What else competes for it?"*
Anchors to real spending behavior and organizational reality. Reveals whether "narrative quality" is actually a purchasing criterion or just a nice-to-have they compliment you about after the fact.

---

## Bonus: The Commitment Ask You're Missing

Every interview should end with a commitment request to separate polite clients from genuinely engaged ones. Add a sixth question:

**Q6: *"I'm building out my next few months. Who in your network is wrestling with something similar — would you be willing to introduce me to one person this week?"***

This tests real advocacy. A referral intro costs them reputation — it's a meaningful commitment. A "let me think about it" is a zombie signal.

## Rules you violated (so you can self-check next time)

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea** — Q1, Q2, Q3, Q5 all ask about you or your thesis.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals** — Q3 and Q4 live in the future; Q5 asks an abstract rating.
3. **Kill the compliment traps** — Q1, Q2, Q5 only have one socially acceptable answer.

Rebuilt properly, these interviews should feel less like "customer testimonials" and more like reconstructing the exact hiring decision, frustration moments, and budget reality of 5 real buyers. That's the data that will actually change how you sell your next project.
