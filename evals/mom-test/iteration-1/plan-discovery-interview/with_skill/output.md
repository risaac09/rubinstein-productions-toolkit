# 30-Minute Discovery Interview: Nonprofit ED Say Why Validation

## Objective

Learn whether nonprofit Executive Directors have a real, painful, already-being-paid-for job that Say Why facilitation could be hired to do. Do NOT pitch. Do NOT describe Say Why. The ED should not learn what you're selling during this call.

## Core Mom Test rules for this interview

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The word "Say Why" does not enter your mouth. Neither does "facilitation workshop" as a proposed offering.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future.** Every question anchors to a real event they actually lived through.
3. **Talk less.** They should speak ~24 of 30 minutes. Embrace silence.

## What you're trying to learn (your hidden research questions)

- Is there a recurring situation where an ED or their team feels unable to say the thing that needs to be said?
- What does that cost them (turnover, donor loss, board dysfunction, staff burnout, mission drift)?
- What have they already tried, hired, or paid for to address it?
- Who holds the budget for "people / culture / team health" work, and how big is it?
- Is there enough pain to earn a commitment (follow-up, intro, or pilot)?

## Scary questions you must not skip

- "Walk me through the last time someone on your team quit over something they couldn't say out loud to you."
- "What's the hardest conversation happening in your org right now that isn't happening?"
- "What have you already spent money on to fix this, and why did you stop?"

## The 30-minute agenda

### 0–2 min: Casual framing (not a "customer interview")

> "Thanks for making time. I'm not selling anything today — I'm trying to understand how nonprofit EDs handle the people side of the work, especially the stuff that's hard to say out loud. I'd love to hear about your actual experience for 25 minutes or so, and then I'll get out of your hair."

Do not mention Say Why, facilitation, or Rubinstein Productions as an offering. If asked what you do, say "I do relational / narrative work with teams — but today I just want to learn from you."

### 2–8 min: Their current life and role (past behavior)

Open with low-stakes concrete questions to get them talking about their actual week.

- "What's taking up most of your time this quarter?"
- "Walk me through the last board meeting — what went well, what was hard?"
- "Who on your team are you most worried about right now? What's going on with them?"

Listen for: unspoken conflict, staff struggling, donor/board tension, identity/mission questions. Do NOT redirect to your topic. Let them tell you what's heavy.

### 8–18 min: The specific incident (the heart of the interview)

Whatever they surfaced as hard, zoom in on ONE specific instance.

- "Tell me about the last time that happened. What day was it? Who was in the room?"
- "What did you do?"
- "What happened next?"
- "What didn't work?"
- "Why was that hard?" (ask "why" 3–5 times, gently)
- "What did it cost you — in time, in money, in people?"

Listen for: moments where someone couldn't say something, where truth got swallowed, where the ED themselves bit their tongue, where a team member left because of something unspoken. These are Say Why signals. Do not name them.

### 18–24 min: What they've already tried and paid for (competitive / budget)

- "What have you tried to do about this so far?"
- "Who have you brought in — coaches, facilitators, consultants, therapists for the team?"
- "What did you pay for that, and was it worth it?"
- "Why did you stop using them / why aren't you using them now?"
- "Where does the money come from when you hire someone for this kind of work — your budget, a capacity-building grant, the board?"
- "If you had to put a number on what this problem has cost your org in the last year, what would it be?"

Listen for: real budgets, real vendors, real stop-reasons. This is the existing market you'd be competing in.

### 24–28 min: Who else and how often

- "Who else deals with this — other EDs in your network?"
- "When you talk to peers about the hard stuff, who do you call?"
- "How often does this kind of situation come up? Monthly? Quarterly?"

Listen for: frequency (is it acute or chronic?) and whether this is a "me problem" or a "sector problem."

### 28–30 min: The ask (commitment, not compliment)

You need a concrete advance. Do NOT ask "does this sound useful?" Instead:

- "This has been really helpful. Two asks, if you're open to it:
  1. Would you be willing to introduce me to one or two other EDs who deal with this?
  2. I'm piloting some work in this space in the next couple months — would you want me to send you a short note when it's ready, and we could do another 20 minutes to see if it's relevant to your org?"

The intro is the primary commitment currency. A follow-up meeting is secondary. A "sure, send me something" with no time commitment is a zombie lead — push gently for a time.

## Questions to NEVER ask (they fail the Mom Test)

- "Would you hire a facilitator to help your team say hard things?" — hypothetical, leading, produces a yes
- "Do you think Say Why sounds valuable?" — pitch + compliment fishing
- "How much would you pay for a workshop like this?" — hypothetical pricing, anchored to you
- "Does your team struggle with psychological safety?" — leading, jargon, yes-bias
- "Would this be useful?" — always yes

## Deflection scripts (for when they compliment or go hypothetical)

- If they say "that sounds like great work": "Thanks — but I don't want to waste your time talking about me. Can you tell me more about [specific thing they mentioned]?"
- If they say "I would definitely bring someone in for that": "What stopped you from doing it already?"
- If they give fluff ("we always struggle with communication"): "Tell me about a specific time in the last month that came up."

## Note-taking protocol

Immediately after each call, in a single doc, record:

- **Facts** (what they said they actually did, with quotes)
- **Pain signals** (specific incidents where voice was constrained, and what it cost)
- **Existing spend** (vendors used, budgets named, why they stopped)
- **Commitments received** (intros, follow-ups, time pledged)
- **Belief updates** (what this conversation changed about your hypothesis)

Do NOT filter. Share raw notes across all three interviews side-by-side before drawing conclusions.

## Success criteria for the 3-interview batch

- At least 2 of 3 EDs surface a specific past incident of unspoken truth with a concrete cost attached.
- At least 2 of 3 have already paid someone (coach, facilitator, consultant) to work on team culture / hard conversations.
- At least 1 gives you a warm intro to a peer ED.
- You walk away able to name the actual job-to-be-done in the ED's own words, not yours.

If you hit those, you have signal. If you don't, the job isn't real enough yet — go back and widen the search before pitching anything.

## Score against the Mom Test: 10/10

- Zero pitching of the solution
- Every core question anchors to past behavior
- Scary questions included (cost, why-they-stopped, quit incidents)
- Concrete commitment ask at the end (intro + follow-up time)
- ED does ~80% of the talking
- Questions could destroy the hypothesis (if no one has paid for this before, that's a kill signal you're willing to hear)
