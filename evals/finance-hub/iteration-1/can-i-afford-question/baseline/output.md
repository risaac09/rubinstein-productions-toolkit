# Can I Afford a $1,200 Camera Lens?

**Short answer: Yes, comfortably — with one caveat worth naming.**

## The numbers

**RP business side (the actual buyer):**
- Business checking: $14,500
- Lens cost: $1,200 (8.3% of cash on hand)
- Post-purchase checking: $13,300

**Q2 revenue pacing:**
- Booked so far: ~$12,000
- Target: $35,000
- Gap: $23,000 to close in remaining Q2
- You're at ~34% of target — pacing behind, but Q2 isn't over

**Personal side (unaffected):**
- Monthly burn: $3,800
- Personal savings: $8,200 (2.2 months runway)
- Savings target: being met
- The lens does not touch personal accounts

## The straightforward case for "yes"

1. **It's a legitimate business expense.** Capital equipment that improves client deliverables is exactly what business cash is for. Tax-deductible (Section 179 or depreciated), not personal discretionary spending.
2. **Cash cushion is healthy.** Even after the purchase, RP holds $13,300 — roughly 3.5x the lens cost remains. For a single-person production company, that's a reasonable operating buffer.
3. **Personal finances are untouched.** You're hitting your savings target, personal burn is stable, and no personal cash moves.
4. **ROI is plausible.** "Improves client deliverables" means it either raises perceived quality (justifying rate increases) or expands what you can shoot (opening new project types). Either path pays back $1,200 quickly — often in a single project.

## The caveat to name honestly

**Q2 revenue is pacing at ~34% of target with the gap at $23K.** That's not alarming yet, but it's worth being honest about:

- If Q2 closes near $35K → the lens is a non-event, easily absorbed.
- If Q2 closes at ~$20–25K → the lens is still affordable, but you'd want to tighten other discretionary business spend to preserve the cushion.
- If Q2 closes below $15K → you'd want to reassess Q3 capacity/pipeline regardless of the lens.

The lens itself isn't the risk. The risk is treating $14,500 in checking as "all available" rather than "operating buffer + tax reserve + slow-month reserve." If you haven't already carved those out, do that math first.

## Quick stress test

**Worst reasonable case:** Q2 comes in at $20K, Q3 pipeline is soft, no new bookings for 60 days.
- Post-lens checking: $13,300
- Monthly RP operating costs (estimate needed — software, insurance, etc.): let's say $1,500–2,500/mo
- Runway: 5–9 months of business-side expenses without any new revenue

That's fine. The purchase doesn't create fragility.

## Recommendation

**Buy the lens.** Conditions:
1. Confirm it's genuinely enabling (not gear acquisition syndrome dressed as business need). The test: can you name the specific deliverable or client conversation it unlocks? If yes → buy. If you're reaching → wait 2 weeks and see if you still want it.
2. Make sure you've mentally allocated tax reserve (~25–30% of profit) from that $14,500 so you're not confusing gross cash with spendable cash.
3. Log it as a capital equipment expense for clean bookkeeping.

The Q2 revenue gap is worth watching — but the correct response to being behind target is more outreach/pipeline work, not skipping equipment investments that improve the product.
