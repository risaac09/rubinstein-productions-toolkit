## Opportunity Triage: Boston Environmental Nonprofit Brand Video

**Verdict: Conditional yes — take it, but reframe the engagement before signing.**

### What works

- $2,500 for a 3-minute brand video is thin but workable for a nonprofit client, especially if production complexity is low (interview-driven, minimal locations, existing b-roll assets from the org).
- Environmental nonprofits are mission-aligned work. If you care about the sector, this builds a portfolio piece and a relationship with an ED who is personally engaged — that matters more than the budget.
- An engaged ED is a green flag. The person who signs off is leaning in. That energy carries through production and makes the final product better.

### What needs attention

**The language gap is the real issue.** "Marketing video" and "brand video" are not the same thing, and if you don't close that gap before signing, you will end up in scope creep or a misaligned deliverable.

- A "marketing video" implies a tool with a specific conversion goal — drive donations, recruit volunteers, push a campaign. The client will expect measurable ROI and may want calls-to-action, stats overlays, or a direct-response tone.
- A "brand video" is about identity, values, and emotional resonance. It is a long-shelf-life asset that says "this is who we are." Different structure, different pacing, different success criteria.

If the ED keeps saying "marketing video," they may be expecting something transactional. You need to find out what problem this video is supposed to solve before you agree on a deliverable.

### Before you say yes

1. **Have one more conversation.** Ask the ED directly: "What does success look like six months after this video goes live? Where will it live — homepage, social, fundraising decks, all of the above?" Their answer tells you which video they actually need.

2. **Name the distinction.** Say something like: "I want to make sure we're building the right thing. A brand video and a marketing video serve different purposes — I'd love to talk through which one actually moves the needle for you right now." This positions you as a strategic partner, not a vendor. It also protects you from delivering a brand piece when they wanted a fundraising ad.

3. **Scope tightly at $2,500.** At this budget, you can deliver a strong product if you constrain:
   - 1 shoot day (half day if possible)
   - 1-2 interview subjects
   - Minimal scripted narration; let real voices carry it
   - 1 round of revisions
   - Final delivery in 2-3 formats (web, social cut, full)

   Make these constraints explicit in the proposal. Do not leave room for "can we also get a 30-second cut and a vertical version and some stills?" without a change order.

4. **Price the upsell path.** If the conversation reveals they actually need a marketing/campaign video too, quote that separately. "We can do the brand video at $2,500 and the campaign cut as an add-on for $X." This protects margins and shows you think in systems, not one-offs.

### Risk factors

- **Scope creep probability: moderate.** Nonprofits often have multiple stakeholders with different visions. The ED is engaged, but a board member or comms director may surface mid-project with a different ask. Tight scope language in the proposal is your insurance.
- **Budget pressure: real but manageable.** $2,500 is below market for a polished 3-minute video in Boston, but it is viable if you are disciplined about shoot logistics and post-production hours. Do not let this become a $4,000 project delivered at a $2,500 price.
- **Relationship upside: significant.** Environmental nonprofits network tightly. A great piece here can lead to referrals across the sector. Treat it as a door-opener, not a one-off.

### Bottom line

Take it if the next conversation confirms alignment on what the video is and what it is for. Walk if the ED cannot articulate a clear purpose or if the scope balloons beyond what $2,500 can support. The language mismatch is not a dealbreaker — it is an invitation to lead the client toward clarity, which is exactly the kind of value that earns repeat work.
