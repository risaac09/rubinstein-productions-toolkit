# Voice Liberation Messaging Angles — Founders in Transition

Mapped across Eugene Schwartz's 5 awareness stages, for founders whose organizations are growing, restructuring, or rebranding.

---

## Stage 1: Unaware

**Angle: "The Founder Voice Drift"**

The founder doesn't know they have a problem yet. They're heads-down in the transition — hiring, restructuring, rebranding — and haven't noticed that somewhere along the way, their voice stopped sounding like theirs. The messaging got "professionalized." The website reads like three other companies in the space. Team members quote the founder's old language, not their current thinking.

*Hook territory:* "Your company is saying things you'd never say out loud." / "The last time your website sounded like you was two pivots ago." / Pattern-interrupt observations about how transitions silently sand down founder voice.

*Goal:* Name a drift they feel but haven't articulated. Create the category in their mind.

---

## Stage 2: Problem-Aware

**Angle: "Transition Eats Voice"**

Now they know something's off. They've heard themselves in a pitch meeting and cringed. They've read their own About page and thought "who wrote this?" They sense that growth/restructure/rebrand is costing them something, but they're framing it as a positioning problem, a copywriting problem, a brand problem.

*Hook territory:* "Growth doesn't dilute your voice. Committee decisions do." / "Rebrands kill founder voice by default — here's why." / Stories of founders who lost their voice during Series A, during a restructure, during an identity refresh.

*Goal:* Reframe what they think the problem is. It's not bad copy. It's that the founder got abstracted out of their own company.

---

## Stage 3: Solution-Aware

**Angle: "Voice Work Isn't Brand Work"**

They now believe voice matters and is recoverable. They're considering options: hiring a brand strategist, a copywriter, a messaging consultant, a fractional CMO. They're about to buy the wrong thing — because brand/messaging/copy work operates at the surface, and what they actually need is deeper: to metabolize what they think, locate what they've stopped saying, and say the thing.

*Hook territory:* "Brand strategists give you words. Voice liberation gives you back your mouth." / "Why messaging frameworks fail founders in transition." / The distinction between external positioning and internal clarity.

*Goal:* Differentiate Voice Liberation from adjacent categories they're about to spend money on. Establish that this is a different *kind* of work.

---

## Stage 4: Product-Aware

**Angle: "Built for the Transition Window"**

They know Voice Liberation exists and that Rubinstein Productions does it. They're comparing: is this for me, right now, at this stage? The objection is usually timing ("after the restructure settles") or fit ("we're too early / too big / too mid-transition"). The angle: transition *is* the window. Voice work done during growth/restructure/rebrand shapes what the next era sounds like. Done after, it's remedial.

*Hook territory:* "Rebrand without voice work and you'll rebrand again in 18 months." / "The best time to do this is the week before the all-hands." / Case framing around founders who did voice work *inside* the transition vs. after.

*Goal:* Collapse the "later" objection. Make the transition itself the reason to start now.

---

## Stage 5: Most Aware

**Angle: "Say The Thing, Before The Town Hall"**

They're sold on the approach, sold on RP, and ready to move. They just need the specific on-ramp that matches their moment. The offer here is concrete and time-shaped: a Voice Liberation engagement scoped to a specific transition artifact — the rebrand launch, the restructure announcement, the Series B narrative, the new-hire onboarding deck — where the founder's actual voice has to show up or the whole thing reads as committee.

*Hook territory:* Direct offers with specificity. "Voice Liberation sprint: 2 weeks, before your rebrand launches." / "Founder voice capture for your restructure announcement." / Named deliverables tied to transition moments.

*Goal:* Close. Turn awareness into a scoped engagement with a near-term deadline.

---

## Stack Logic

The stages laddering: drift noticed → named as voice loss → distinguished from brand work → timed to the transition → scoped to a specific artifact. Each angle hands the founder to the next stage without skipping steps. The Unaware hook has to pattern-interrupt; the Most Aware offer has to be dated and priced.
